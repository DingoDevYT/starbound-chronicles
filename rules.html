Core Rulebook

Starbound Chronicles: Core Rulebook

Alpha v1.0 — The foundational mechanical framework for players and GMs alike. Fast-paced action, cooperative crew dynamics, and an unrestrictive open-world structure.

Alpha v1.0 d20 System Grid Combat Open Sandbox
Section 01

Game Overview & Philosophy

Starbound Chronicles is designed to prioritize fast-paced action, collaborative crew dynamics, and an unrestrictive, open-world structure. Unlike traditional campaign-driven RPGs, Starbound Chronicles utilizes a modular design built to interact seamlessly with an evolving digital sector map. The core philosophy centers on a shared asset: your crew's starship.

GM's Note

The GM sets the scene, plays the factions, and controls the living galaxy. Players are the crew — each commanding their station, pooling resources, and making decisions that ripple across the open map. No railroading. No predetermined story. Just the void and whatever trouble you fly into.

Section 02

The Core Engine — The Four "F"s

All actions in Starbound Chronicles — whether traversing a lawless space station on foot or exchanging laser fire with a pirate cruiser — are resolved using a d20 system governed by four primary statistics. Ditch traditional metrics of physical or mental attributes; here, your capabilities are defined by operational functions.

FLY
Manual & Movement
Governs manual dexterity, piloting maneuvers, evasion, reflexes, and physical stealth.
FIX
Technical & Scientific
Governs mechanical engineering, software hacking, weapon modifications, and scientific scanning.
FIGHT
Combat & Force
Governs physical force, personal firearms proficiency, and targeting heavy ship-mounted turrets.
FACE
Social & Electronic
Governs social manipulation, deception, intimidation, trading, and electronic communication warfare.
Section 02 — Resolution

Resolution Mechanics

When a character attempts an action with a risk of failure, they roll a 20-sided die (d20) and add the relevant stat modifier. If the total meets or exceeds the Difficulty Class (DC) set by the GM, the action succeeds. Modifiers are calculated as (stat − 10) ÷ 2, rounded down.

DC Classification Example Actions
10 Easy Bypassing a standard civilian lock · Landing a ship in clear weather · Bribing a low-tier guard
15 Moderate Slicing into a secure corporate network · Executing a combat barrel roll · Negotiating with a rival captain
20 Hard Disabling a military-grade tracking array mid-combat · Haggling with a corrupt sector warlord · Hot-landing on a moving platform
Advantage & Disadvantage

When circumstances strongly favor success, the GM may grant Advantage — roll 2d20 and take the higher result. When circumstances work against the character, they roll at Disadvantage — roll 2d20 and take the lower result. Advantage and Disadvantage cancel each other out regardless of how many sources are stacked.

Section 03

Classes & Crew Roles

Your class defines your specialization on foot and dictates your primary station when operating the crew's collective starship. Each class focuses on one of the Four Fs and unlocks a Core Feature — a unique ability that changes how your ship station operates.

Class Stat Focus Ship Station Core Feature
👨‍✈️ The Captain FACE The Bridge Tactical Directives: Grants a flat bonus to any crew member's station check during the Action Phase.
🚀 The Pilot FLY The Helm Evasive Manoeuvre: Throw the ship into a violent drift to boost its total AD for the round.
🔫 The Gunsmith FIGHT Weapons Array Overcharge Salvo: Doubles the weapon damage dice pool at the cost of aim accuracy.
⚙️ The Technician FIX Engineering Delta System Reboot: Clears stuns and other negative effects from all ship components.
Multiclassing Note

At GM discretion, crew members may train secondary station roles over the course of a campaign. A Gunsmith who spends downtime at the helm under a Pilot's instruction may unlock limited access to Helm maneuvers — at a reduced power cost efficiency.

Section 04

Combat

Combat comes in two forms, and both run on the same spine: initiative order, a d20 vs Aim Difficulty (AD) hit roll, and a deliberately tiny action economy. There are no reactions, no bonus actions, and no spell slots — that complexity has been stripped out on purpose. You have a gun. If it's equipped, you shoot. You move. That's the game.

🔫 On-Foot Combat
Every PC and NPC is their own token and rolls their own initiative. Your entire offense is your one equipped weapon.
🚀 Spacecraft Combat
The whole party crews a single vessel token. Each ship rolls one initiative; the crew win or lose it together through their stations.

4.1 · The Hit System (AD)

To attack, roll d20 + FIGHT modifier + weapon accuracy. If the total meets or beats the target's Total AD, you hit. A higher AD is harder to hit.

Total AD = Base AD + Distance + Cover

Every point of AD is one more you need on the d20. Distance is the big one — the further away the target, the harder the shot.

Base AD — by size

Base AD is purely about how big a target is. Small things are hard to hit; big things are easy.

TargetSizeBase AD
SkitterlingTiny10
Human · MyxosMedium8
ArboranTall7
KragolLarge6
Sleek InterceptorSmall ship14
Standard FrigateMedium ship10
Heavy Cargo HaulerLarge ship6

Distance — +1 AD per 20 m

Each grid square is 10 m. Every 20 m (2 squares) between shooter and target adds +1 AD. The in-app ruler tool measures any two points and tells you the exact distance and AD penalty. Point-blank is trivial; a 200 m shot is a real gamble.

DistanceAD penaltyvs an AD-8 human, +3 shooter
20 m+1AD 9 — hit on 6+ (~75%, easy)
100 m+5AD 13 — hit on 10+ (~55%)
200 m+10AD 18 — hit on 15+ (~30%, hard)
300 m+15AD 23 — need a 20 (~5%, brutal)

Cover & Armor

🪨 Cover: +4 AD
Tuck a token against an obstacle (asteroid, rock) between it and the shooter and it gains +4 AD. Obstacles are solid — you can't move through them. The Pilot's Thread the Needle boosts this further.
🛡️ Armor: flat damage cut
Armor does not make you harder to hit — it subtracts straight from the damage of every hit that lands. Armor 3 turns a 10-damage hit into 7.

4.2 · Initiative & the Turn

Everyone (or every ship) rolls initiative once: d20 + movement, where movement is your speed in squares (see below). Highest goes first. On your turn you get exactly one Movement action and one Core action — nothing else.

▸
Movement Action
Move up to speed ÷ 5 squares (a speed-30 character moves 6 squares = 60 m). Obstacles block the path.
◆
Core Action — pick one
Attack with your equipped weapon (roll vs AD) · Dash to move again at half your movement · Switch weapon (the only way to change what's equipped — it eats your whole Core action).
Why so bare?

No opportunity attacks, no reactions, no bonus actions, no slots. If you want to shoot, have the right gun equipped. If you don't, spend a turn switching. Move, then do one thing. That's the whole loop — for a person or a starship.

4.3 · On-Foot Combat

Each PC and NPC is a token on a gridded map (a plain, a rocky canyon, ruins…). Your one equipped weapon is your entire offense — its damage dice roll on a hit, then the target's Armor is subtracted. Want a different weapon? Switching is a Core action, so you'll skip a shot to do it. Carry your loadout wisely.

Your turn, at a glance

During combat the app shows the active character a cheat-sheet: your movement in squares, your equipped weapon's to-hit and damage, and your three Core options. No rulebook lookups mid-fight.

4.4 · Spacecraft Combat

Instead of individual tokens, the party crews one vessel token. The ship rolls a single initiative (d20 + its speed in squares) and acts as a unit, powered by the shared Power Point (PP) pool from its reactor. Each crew member drives their station — see Crew Roles for every class action and its PP cost.

🛡️
Shields
First to take hits
All incoming damage drains Shield HP first. The Technician can restore them mid-fight.
🚢
Hull
The real health
Once Shields hit 0, damage leaks into Hull HP. At 0 Hull the ship is crippled or destroyed.
⚡
PP
Power Points
Refresh each round from the reactor. Every station action spends PP, so the crew must budget together.
Damage: Shields, then Hull

A hit that rolls, say, 14 damage against a ship with 8 Shields leaves the shields at 0 and carries the remaining 6 into the Hull. Both bars can be damaged and healed independently in the app — the Technician's job is keeping that shield bar between the enemy and the hull.

Section 05

Open-World Living Map & Factions

Starbound Chronicles relies on a decentralized sandboxed map. Sectors are assigned Security Ratings and Faction Influence Maps, altering encounter rates and economic values dynamically. Where you fly determines what you risk — and what you stand to gain.

Security Level Classification Economic Modifier Risk Assessment
Sec-5 to Sec-4 High-Sec
Imperial / Military Core		 Standard legal market rates; heavy contraband fine multiplier. Minimal piracy. High local police intervention if laws are breached.
Sec-3 to Sec-1 Low-Sec
The Borderlands / Fringe		 Increased value for rare minerals and black-market items. Moderate pirate activity; law enforcement arrival delayed by 1d4 rounds.
Sec-0 Null-Sec
The Wild Expanse		 Maximum credit payouts; lawless trading parameters. Total anarchy. Extreme hazard zones. Unknown alien threats.
Faction Influence

Beyond security ratings, sectors are contested by major factions — the Imperial Compact, the Free Traders' Union, the Syndicate, and the Void Collective. Faction influence affects prices, quest availability, and which ports will grant you docking rights. Completing faction jobs shifts the influence map, and the results persist across all sessions in the living world.
